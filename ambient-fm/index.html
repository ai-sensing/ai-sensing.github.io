Abstract

Ambient intelligence—continuously understanding human presence, activity, and physiology in physical spaces—is fundamental to smart environments, health monitoring, and human–computer interaction. WiFi infrastructure provides a ubiquitous, privacy-preserving substrate for this capability across billions of IoT devices, yet wireless sensing has typically relied on task-specific models that need substantial labeled data and limit deployment.

We present AmbientFM, the first foundation model for ambient intelligence and sensing through WiFi. AmbientFM is pre-trained on 9.2 million unlabeled channel state information (CSI) samples collected over 439 days from 20 commercial device types deployed worldwide, using contrastive learning, masked reconstruction, and physics-informed objectives tailored to wireless signals. On public benchmarks spanning nine downstream tasks, AmbientFM achieves strong cross-task performance with improved data efficiency, showing that foundation models can scale ambient sensing on existing wireless infrastructure.

Why AmbientFM?

WiFi CSI captures how signals propagate through indoor environments: motion and physiology modulate channel responses without cameras or wearables. Commodity hardware already produces massive unlabeled CSI streams, but prior work mostly trains separate specialized models per task, so knowledge does not transfer and labeled data must be collected again for each application.

AmbientFM targets general-purpose CSI representations learned from diverse in-the-wild data. The encoder combines adaptive frequency aggregation (cross-attention over heterogeneous subcarriers), a transformer backbone with relative temporal position bias, and three complementary self-supervised losses that reflect multipath structure, packet loss, and temporal periodicities in real propagation.

Downstream adaptation uses either a frozen encoder with a lightweight temporal probe (1-layer LSTM + head) or bottleneck adapters (~393K trainable parameters, <8% of encoder size) inserted in each transformer layer—both paired with the same temporal classifier for fair comparison.

Compared to training the same architecture from scratch, pre-training helps most on tasks that require cross-environment invariance (for example human activity recognition, localization, occupancy, and proximity), while highly discriminative tasks such as user identification already achieve strong performance with limited labels.

Framework

Continuous CSI collection → WiFi-aware self-supervised pre-training → parameter-efficient adaptation to diverse sensing tasks.

Method

Architecture (Base)

  • Adaptive frequency aggregation: cross-attention maps variable subcarriers to 10 latent frequency channels per spatial slice (~5–11× compression), emphasizing informative bins under heterogeneous fading and hardware.
  • Temporal encoder: 6-layer transformer, hidden size 256, 8 heads, with learned relative position bias for translation-invariant activity patterns.
  • Pre-training decoder: 3-layer transformer for reconstruction; segment shape after preprocessing is typically T = 500 time steps and unified frequency bins (zero-padded to Fmax).
  • Scale: Small ~2M, Base ~5M, Large ~12M parameters; Base achieves the best overall transfer on the fixed 9.2M-sample corpus.

Self-supervised objectives

  • Contrastive (NT-Xent): temporal pooling + projector; nine WiFi-specific augmentations (noise, gain-style modulation, temporal shift, frequency perturbation/mask, crop, blur, …)—standard image-style augmentations hurt temporal tasks.
  • Masked reconstruction: masks 10% temporal blocks and 10% frequency bands; decoder reconstructs the full segment to capture multipath coherence.
  • Physics-informed ACF: auxiliary prediction of normalized autocorrelation of per-timestep signal energy, aligning representations with periodic motion statistics.

Pre-processing uses CSI amplitude, fixed-length windows, per-segment min–max normalization, and HDF5 storage for heterogeneous devices (details in the paper).

Pre-training corpus

The pre-training set is collected 24/7 in residential settings with 6–9 concurrent device links per environment, emphasizing realistic motion, occlusion, and interference (not scripted lab sessions only).

Coverage

  • 26 participants · 11 environments (studios to multi-floor homes, ~576–3,800 sq ft)
  • 20 IoT device types, 33 physical devices
  • 8 chipset families (e.g. MediaTek, Qualcomm, Broadcom, Espressif, NXP, Marvell, Realtek, Altobeam)
  • Antennas 1×1–2×2, bandwidths 20 / 40 / 80 MHz, 2.4 & 5 GHz

Quality control

  • Target 100 Hz packet rate; streams below ⅔ of target rate are filtered.
  • Devices screened with a MATLAB CSI verification workflow for timing stability, amplitude behavior, and motion sensitivity before large-scale logging.
  • Received power spread can exceed 20 dB across configurations—motivating learned frequency weighting.

Nine downstream tasks

Evaluated on public benchmarks: CSI-Bench (broad in-the-wild tasks), SignFi (276-class gestures), and WiFiCam (CSI–RGB imaging).

Physiological

Fall detection — acute events from transient channel disruptions.

Activity

Human activity recognition (HAR) · Motion source recognition (MSR) (human vs pet vs robot vs fan).

Spatial reasoning

Occupancy · Localization · Proximity (fine-grained distance classes).

Biometrics

User identification (UID) from gait-like motion signatures.

Fine-grained motion

Gesture recognition — 276-way SignFi classification.

Spatial reconstruction

WiFi imaging — dense prediction; reported as SSIM / PSNR (not AUROC).

With BA, AmbientFM exceeds 0.90 AUROC on all eight classification tasks in the paper’s Table 1. Temporal probing is especially strong when classes are already well separated in embedding space (UID, MSR, localization). Few-shot curves (paper Fig. 2) show large gains on localization and proximity where scratch models remain near chance with limited labels. Ablations (paper Fig. 3) show both WiFi-tailored augmentations and the full three-part loss are important—dropping reconstruction or ACF hurts especially fall detection and HAR.

Deployment

The Base encoder (~5.57M parameters, ~21.3 MB) runs at about 4.25 µs per sample and 233k+ samples/s on a commodity laptop CPU (AMD Ryzen 7 PRO 7840U) without a GPU—sufficient for real-time edge inference after adaptation.

Limitations (summary): each task still needs a short adaptation stage; performance gains vary by task. Robustness degrades under sustained packet loss ≳50% or SNR below roughly 15 dB in the paper’s analysis. AmbientFM senses what is encoded in commodity CSI—not fine visual appearance.

Contributions

  1. Foundation model for ambient sensing: First large-scale WiFi CSI foundation model aimed at ambient intelligence, with architecture matched to multipath and hardware heterogeneity.
  2. Real-world pre-training at scale: Nine complementary objectives and augmentations tailored to wireless physics, trained on the largest diverse in-the-wild CSI corpus described in the paper.
  3. Single backbone, nine tasks: Strong transfer and label efficiency across physiological, activity, spatial, biometric, gesture, and imaging tasks using shared representations and lightweight adaptation.
